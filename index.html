Reaffirmation Agreements

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c) -- the process of voluntarily agreeing to remain liable for a debt that would otherwise be wiped out in Chapter 7 bankruptcy.

What Is a Reaffirmation Agreement?

A reaffirmation agreement is a voluntary contract between a debtor and a creditor in which the debtor agrees to remain personally liable for a debt that would otherwise be discharged in Chapter 7 bankruptcy. In exchange, the debtor typically gets to keep the collateral securing the debt -- usually a car or, in some cases, a home.

Reaffirmation is most common with car loans. The lender agrees to let you keep making payments and keep driving the vehicle. In return, you give up the protection of the discharge for that particular debt. You keep the property, but you also keep the full obligation -- including any deficiency balance if things go wrong later.

The agreement must be filed with the bankruptcy court before the discharge is entered. Once signed and filed, it is legally binding unless you exercise your right to rescind within 60 days.

When Reaffirmation Makes Sense

You need the vehicle. If you depend on a car for work, family, or medical needs, and replacing it would cost more than continuing the current loan, reaffirmation can be the practical choice. This is especially true when the loan balance is close to or below the vehicle's market value.

The lender requires it. Some lenders -- particularly for car loans -- will repossess the vehicle after discharge unless you sign a reaffirmation agreement, even if you are current on payments. Whether they can legally do this depends on the circuit you are in, but the practical reality is that many lenders enforce this policy.

You want to rebuild credit. Reaffirmed debts continue to report to the credit bureaus. If you make consistent payments, this can help rebuild your credit score after bankruptcy. Debts that are discharged without reaffirmation may stop reporting entirely.

Timing matters. The reaffirmation agreement must be filed with the court before the discharge order is entered. If you miss this window, the option is off the table. Your attorney (or you, if pro se) must act promptly after filing.

The Risks

Reaffirmation carries real financial risk that every debtor should understand before signing.

The key difference: Without reaffirmation, if you default, the creditor can repossess the collateral but cannot sue you for the deficiency -- the gap between what you owe and what the property sells for. With reaffirmation, the creditor can repossess and sue you for the deficiency. This is a significant difference.

Consider a car worth $12,000 with a $18,000 loan balance. If you reaffirm and later default, the lender repossesses the car, sells it at auction for $8,000, and sues you for the remaining $10,000. Without reaffirmation, the lender could repossess but could not come after you for that $10,000.

Reaffirmation essentially puts you back in the same position you were in before bankruptcy -- at least for that one debt. If your financial situation is unstable, reaffirming an underwater loan can leave you worse off than if you had surrendered the property and started fresh.

Court Approval -- Section 524(c) Requirements

Congress built safeguards into Section 524(c) to protect debtors from making reaffirmation agreements they cannot afford. The requirements include:

No undue hardship. The agreement must not impose an undue hardship on the debtor or the debtor's dependents. The court looks at your income, expenses, and whether you can realistically make the payments.

Informed consent. The debtor must receive clear disclosures about the consequences of reaffirmation, including the right to rescind and the fact that the debt will survive the discharge.

Court hearing for unrepresented debtors. If the debtor does not have an attorney, the court must hold a hearing and approve the reaffirmation agreement. The judge will review whether the agreement is in the debtor's best interest and whether the debtor can afford the payments. Judges routinely deny reaffirmation agreements when the numbers do not work.

Attorney certification. If the debtor is represented, the attorney must sign a declaration stating that the agreement does not impose an undue hardship, that the debtor was fully informed, and that the debtor entered the agreement voluntarily. This shifts the gatekeeping role from the court to the attorney.

Alternatives to Reaffirmation

Reaffirmation is not the only option for keeping property in Chapter 7. Depending on your circuit and circumstances, you may have better choices.

Ride-through. In some circuits, you can simply keep making payments on a secured debt without signing a reaffirmation agreement. You keep the property and keep paying, but you are not personally liable if you later default. The creditor can repossess if you stop paying, but cannot pursue a deficiency judgment. This is the best of both worlds -- but not all circuits allow it, and not all lenders accept it.

Redemption under Section 722. You can redeem personal property by paying the creditor the current market value of the collateral in a single lump-sum payment. If you owe $18,000 on a car worth $12,000, you pay $12,000 and own the car free and clear. The remaining $6,000 is discharged. The challenge is coming up with the lump sum, though some companies specialize in redemption financing.

Surrender. You give the property back to the creditor, and the debt -- including any deficiency -- is discharged. This is the cleanest option if the property is not worth keeping or if the loan is significantly underwater. It lets you walk away with a true fresh start on that obligation.

The 60-Day Rescission Right

Section 524(c)(4) gives you an important safety valve: you can change your mind and cancel a reaffirmation agreement within 60 days after it is filed with the court, or at any time before the discharge is entered -- whichever is later.

To rescind, you must notify the creditor in writing that you are rescinding the agreement. Once rescinded, the debt is treated as if the reaffirmation agreement was never signed, and the discharge applies to it.

This rescission right exists because Congress recognized that debtors may feel pressured into reaffirmation agreements and should have time to reconsider. If you are having second thoughts after signing, do not wait -- exercise this right promptly.
